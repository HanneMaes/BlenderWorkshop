👁️ Materials

Creating a grid of objects

First we will make a grid of objects to test out different materials.

  1. Remove everything in the Scene: Select > All → Object > Delete or A → X.
  1. Add a Suzanne: Add > Mesh > Monkey or Shift A → Mesh > Monkey.

    Suzanne is the name of Blender's monkey moscotte.

    Now we want to get rid off the rectangular polygon look: Object > Shade Smooth.

  1. Make a grid of Suzannes.

    Duplicate Suzanne

    1. Select Suzanne
    1. Object > Duplicate Objects or Shift D.
    1. Press X the move the new Object along the X-axis.
    1. Type the amount of meters you want the copy to move along the X-axis.

    When workings with these combinations of shortcuts, it's easy to get lost. You can always see what you are doing in the upper left corner of the Viewport.

    In this example we are duplicating (D) 3 meters along the X-axis.

    Repeat unit you have a grid of Suzannes.

    All the new objects that we created will become visible in the Scene Collection.

Creating materials

  1. In order to see the materials we need to select Viewport Shading: Matering Preview at the top right of the Viewport.
  1. Then go to Material Properties
  1. Press + New to create a new material. When a new material is created you can start tweaking it.

    There a lots of settings you can play around with, but these are the most important:

    • Base Color
    • Metallic
    • Specular The white reflection dots and lines
    • Roughness Makes the specular reflection more sharp or diffuse

✏️ Create your own materials

Now try filling up as many of your Suzannes with custom materials. Try to replicate materials like: gold, silver, bronze, chrome, plastic, porcelain, rubber, ...

Advanced Materials

Fill up the other Suzannes with materials made of the other properties.

These are some interesting properties to play with.

  • Subsurface, Subsurface Radius & Subsurface Color
  • Sheen & Sheen Tint
  • Transmission

Try making materials like: glass, mirror, translucent skin, materials that emit light, velvet, ...

👁️ Viewport Shading

In the upper right corner you can select the Wiewport Shading mode. With Viewport Shading you can change how the scene is displayed. There are 4 modes.

  • Wireframe This mode displays the internal structure of objects.
  • Solid This mode displays your scene without lights, materials, ... This is the default mode because it lets you focus on the shapes as is rendered very fast so your scene does not lag.
  • Material Preview This mode displays your scene with materials applied, but without lights.
  • Rendered This mode displays your scene how it will be rendered.

👁️ Scene backgrounds

High-dynamic-range images (HDRI)

You properly notices that the Rendered Viewport Shading looks weird and ugly. That's because the World is grey, and the reflective materials have nothing the reflect. This problem can be solved in 2 ways.

This first way is to put other objects in the scene and make the environment yourself.

The second, and most easy way is the use HDR images. These images are specially made to mimic background scenes. One HDR image contains multiple pictures of the same scene with different exposures, and the ones we use for 3D rendering are shot in 360°.

Downloading HDR images

HDRI Haven is a great website from downloading HDR images.

On HDRI Haven you can download the images at different resolutions. The higher the resolution the better the quality, but also the longer your scenes will take to render. I tend to donwload the 4K resolution.

You can recognize HDR images with their .hdr extention.

✏️ Download a least 2 different HDR images so we can compare them.

Using HDR images

Choose Rendered Viewport Shading.

In the Cartoon workshop we learned how to give the background a color. Go the the World Properties panel.

Instead of using a Color we'll be using a Environment Texture. Click on the little circle next the the color and select Environment Texture.

You can:

  • Choose an Environment Texture that is already in our scene by clicking the image icon.
  • Make a new Environment Texture by clicking + New.
  • Open an Environment Texture by clicking Open.

Choose Open and select an Environment Texture you downloaded from HDRI Haven.

dNow our materials have some environment to reflect. The scene will also be lit by the Environment Texture. Bright spots (like the sun) will emit light and light the scene in a realistic way.

A different Environment Texture will dramatically change the mood of your render.

Click the Folder icon to change to another HDR image.

👁️ Bloom

To make your renders even more realistic, you can use an effect called Bloom. Bloom will make light more realistic by showing it's rays.

No Bloom

Bloom

Open the Render Properties panel and enable Bloom.

Bloom settings

These are the most important Bloom settings.

  • Intensity The Intensity defines show visible the Bloom effect is.
  • Radius The Radius defines how far the light rays travel.
